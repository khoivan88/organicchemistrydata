The Li/Se and Li/Te Exchanges

The Li/Se is a little slower than the Li/Br exchange, similarly for the Li/Te and Li/I exchanges. There are two groups on the chalcogne precursor, only one of which will be subject to exchange, so proper choice of non-tranfering group is important - PhSeR and PhTeR compounds may be the easiest to prepare, but PhLi is a potential exchange product. The byproduct selenides and tellurides are substantially less reactive than iodides or bromides in S2 substitutions and eliminations, which can be an advantage in some situations.

  
  

The Li/Te exchange shows excellent generality and is almost as fast as the Li/I exchange. Because of the higher expense and the difficulty of working with some tellurium compounds, the method has not been used much in synthesis. For some systems, as in the streospecific preparation of isomeric cyclohexyllithiums below, it works better than any other method (Reich, H. J.; Medina, M. A.; Bowe, M. D. J. Am. Chem. Soc. 1992, 114, 11003-11004).

The Li/Sn Exchange

  

The Li/Sn exchange is in some ways the most reliable of the transmetalation reactions. The Me3Sn or Bu3Sn groups do not acidify a- and b-protons very much, so metalation is rarely a problem, as is sometimes the case with the metal halogen exchanges. The byproduct R4Sn compounds rarely interfere with subsequent reactions, although Bu4Sn can occasionally cause separaion problems (Me4Sn is very volatile and easily removed). The disadvantage of Sn precursors is that trialkyl and tetraalkyl tin compunds are neurotoxic, and must be handled with care (especially the volatile Me3SnX compounds). The Li/Sn exchanges are quite sensitive to steric effects and are considerably slower than Li/I exchanges, so the exchanges can be inconveniently slow if a hindered lithium reagent is being prepared.

  

a-Aminoalkyllithium reagents cannot usually be prepared by the metal-halogen exchange, and the Li/Sn exchange is the best method. D. J. Peterson, J. Am. Chem. Soc. 1971, 93, 4027.

  

a-Alkoxy lithium reagents are also very commonly prepared by Li/Sn exchange. The a-alkoxy tin compounds are easily prepared by reaction of R3SnLi with aldehydes or ketones, or with a-haloethers. N. Meyer, D. Seebach, Chem. Ber. 1980, 113, 1290.
