Aliphatic Claisen Rearrangement

  The aliphatic Claisen (oxa-Cope), in contrast to the all-carbon Cope, has the advantage that the equilibrium constant is reliably favorable in the direction of the carbonyl compound. Claisen himself discovered the earliest example of [3.3]sigmatropic rearrangement of an allyl enol ether: Claisen Chem. Ber. 1912, 42, 3157

 

Several effective protocols to synthesize the required allyl enol ethers have been developed: the enol ether Claisen, the Johnson orthoester Claisen, and the Ireland silyl ketene acetal Claisen.

The Enol Ether-Claisen Process (Marbet-Saucy reaction)

The Ireland Silyl Ketene Acetal Claisen Process

New Aspects of the Ireland and Related Claisen Rearrangements,
  Chai, Y.; Hong, S.-P.; Lindsay, H. A.; McFarland, C.; McIntosh, M. C. Tetrahedron 2002, 58, 2905-28.

  The Ireland modification of the Claisen rearrangement is the mildest and most general of the procedures available. An allyl or propargyl alcohol is converted to an ester, and the ester is deprotonated (typically with LiNiPr2) and O-silylated to form the ketene acetal, which rearranges under mild conditions. The controlled enolization process allows much better stereocontrol of the enol ether formed than is possible with other methods.

Synthesis of Mesembrine: Keck, G. E.; Webb, R. R. J. Org. Chem. 1982, 47, 1302.

Synthetic Equivalance of Claisen Rearrangements

  The Claisen Rearrangement can be thought of as the SN2' alkylation of an enolate by an allyl alcohol. The reaction occurs with complete regiocontol (the position of the alcohol determines the point of attachment), and, in addition has valuable sterreochemical features to be discussed below that are not present in enolate alkylations

 

  Note that this particular alkylation cannot be done by enolate chemistry under any conditions, since the alkylation of a suitably active prenyl halide would not give the product shown, but rather alkylation at the primary allylic position. In fact, the introduction of allyl groups at the more hindered terminus is one of the important uses of Claisen processes.

Chrysanthemic-Acid: Babler, J. H,; Tortorello, A. J. J. Org. Chem. 1976, 41, 885.

 

   The aldehyde version of the Claisen rearangement has no direct synthetic equivalent in enolate chemistry, since aldehyde enolates are generally not suitable for alkylation reactions due to self-condensation problems.
