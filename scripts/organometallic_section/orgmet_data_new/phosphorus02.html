Methylenation with Phosphorus Ylids and Phosphonate Carbanions.

The reactions of α-phosphorus nucleophiles with carbonyl compounds are reliable methods for the synthesis of olefins. There are two principal variants: the Wittig reaction, which uses phosphonium ylids as nucleophiles, and Horner-Wadsworth-Emmons reaction, which uses metalated phosphonates.

The Wittig Reaction

The parent Wittig reagent, methylenetriphenylphosphorane, is perhaps the most commonly used one, even though there are now a number of effective alternative reagents for methylenation of ketones (Tebbe, Takai-Lombardo, Petasis reagents). Wittig reagents of the unstabilized type (Ph3P=CR2, R = H, alkyl) react with aldehydes, ketones, and with some activated esters (formates, trifluoromethyl ketones). Stabilized Wittig reagents bearing a carbanion-stabilizing substituent (R = COH, COR', CO2R') react well with aldehydes, but usually not with less reactive carbonyl compounds.

Wittig reagents bearing one alkyl substituents on the ylidic carbon have a strong tendency to form cis olefins (>90% in many cases, if proper reaction conditions are used). The example below is from a synthesis of Argentilactone (Carretero, J. J.; Ghosez, L Tetrahedron Lett. 1988, 29, 2059). Also commonly used are aryl and vinyl-substituted ylids, for which mixtures of cis and trans olefins are typically seen.

  

Dialkyl-substituted Wittig reagents react poorly or not at all with enolizable ketones, but aldehydes usually work well. M. Terada, K. Mikami Chem. Commun. 1995, 2391.

  
  

Homologation of Aldehydes with Methoxymethylenephosphorane

A common method for the conversion of aldehydes to the next higher homolog utilizes Ph3P=CHOMe (Overman J. Am. Chem. Soc. 1995, 117, 10391).

  

In sterically hindered cases, the Wittig reaction fails. An alternative uses Ph2PCHLiOMe - the phosphonium salt is generated by P-alkylation after carbonyl addition (Wender, Tetrahedron Lett. 1982, 23, 1871)
