--- title: 'Overview of Enolate Alkylations' ---

Overview of Enolate Alkylations

  Ketones, carboxylic esters, carboxylic acids, and carboxamides can in general be converted to enolates by deprotonation with strong bases like lithium diisopropylamide. The enolates can also be formed by conjugate reduction reactions. The enolates react usefully with SN2-reactive alkyl halides, aldehydes, acylating agents, and Michael acceptors, as well as other electrophiles. Aldehyde enolates cannot usually be prepared and used in this way because self-condensation is too fast. The enolates of acid chlorides and acid anhydrides are unstable - they eliminate chloride or acetate to form ketenes.


 

Typical alkylating agents:

  Enolate alkylations usually work with unhindered primary alkyl iodides, and especially well with Sn2 reactive substrates like methyl, allyl, benzyl and propargyl iodides and bromides, chloromethyl ethers and α-halo esters. Iodides are substantially more reactive than bromides and especially chlorides, so they are preferred. Tosylates and other sulfonates can be used, they are comparable in reactivity to bromides. Very reactive sulfonates (e.g. trifluoromethanesulfonates) are faster, but show increased tendency for O-alkylation of enolates.


 

Borderline alkylating agents:

  Epoxides and secondary halides can sometimes be used, but their SN2 reactions with enolates are often too slow to compete with side reaction like elimination of halide to alkene or enolate equilibration (for ketone enolates) and decomposition (for ester enolates).


 

Alkylating agents that do not work:

  Vinyl, aryl and tertiary alkyl halides do not undergo SN2 substitutions at useful rates, and cannot be used for alkali metal enolate alkylations. However, there are reactions in which such electrophiles can be used that either proceed by SN1 mechanisms or by non-SN2 mechanisms involving transition metal catalysts.


 

Ketone Enolates

Equilibrating conditions

  Ketone enolates generally undergo slow intermolecular aldol condensation with their ketone precursors, and formation of the aldol products is often thermodynamically unfavorable - only when conditions are harsh enough to cause dehydration of the aldol products to enones does the reaction become thermodynamically favorable. A crucial factor which restricts the range of electrophiles to the more active ones (aldehydes, MeI, Allyl-X, etc) is the occurrence of proton transfer reactions between the enolate starting reactant and the ketone starting material or products, which can lead to loss of regiospecificity for ketones which can form more than one enolate, and polyalkylation products.

Preformed Enolates

  More commonly it is important that a single regioisomer is formed, and that only a single alkylation occurs. To achieve this, the ketone is first enolized with a strong base like lithium diisopropylamide under conditions of kinetic control, and the alkylation is performed on the enolate solution.

Aldehyde enolates

  It is rare to see successful reactions involving preformed aldehyde enolates. The aldol reaction (either between the starting aldehyde and its enolate, or between the starting enolate and the aldehyde products) is usually too fast to allow successful reactions. Several alternative methods are used instead. The most common uses a carboxylic acid, ester, amide or nitrile enolate, followed by reduction of the carboxylate product to aldehyde. Alternatively, aldehyde azaenolates have much better properties and can be used for all of the common reaction of enolates.

Allyl Nucleophiles as Protected Aldehydes:

  Allyl organometallic reagents are effective nucleophiles (commonly used are Li, Mg, B, Si, Sn), with the additional benefit that often very impressive stereochemical control is seen. The allyl group can be considered a protected aldehyde, to be released at an opportune time by ozonolysis or other C=C bond cleaving reactions (OsO4-NaIO4)

Carboxylic Ester Enolates

  Carboxylic esters with α-protons can usually be deprotonated with lithium diisopropylamide or related bases to form enolates. These enolates are considerably more basic and reactive than ketone enolates, but they are also prone to eliminate to form ketenes, which requires that they be used at relatively low temperatures, and cannot be used with poorly reactive SN2 substrates. The t-butyl esters are stable to room temperature in solution, the methyl and ethyl esters decompose below 0 °C. The ketenes formed in the decomposition usually react immediately with remaining ester enolate to form β-keto esters (Claisen condensation).


 

 Lithium ester enolates will usually give reasonable yields with reactive SN2 substrates (MeI, primary allyl halides, etc), and react well in aldol reactions with aldehydes and ketones.

Carboxylic acid enolate dianions

  Carboxylic acids with α-protons can be doubly deprotonated to form nucleophilic dianions. The enolates are very stable to decomposition, and can be used to perform difficult SN2 substitutions. A common problem is the precipitation of the first-formed anion, the lithium carboxylate, which sometimes slows or prevents the second deprotonation to form the enolate.

β-Dicarbonyl Dianions

  β-Diketones, β-ketoesters and related compounds can be bis-deprotonated to form very nucleophilic dianions, which react mainly at the teminal end of the π-system (α to the ketone). The original procedure used NaH to first produce the stabilized enolate, followed by treatment with n-BuLi to remove the second, much less acidic proton. Later it was shown that 2 equiv. of LiNiPr2 will perform both deprotonations.


 

  In addition to 1,3-dicarbonyl compounds, other related bifunctional compounds can also form dianions of this type. Each of the dianions react mainly at the ketone enolate (left) end of the system.
